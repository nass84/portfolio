1 The foundation

Iternal: designing for children in hospital

My first UX role after School of Code was at Iternal — a startup building digital patient diaries for children and their parents during hospital stays. The idea was simple and human: while in hospital, children and families could tell their stories, share them with loved ones, and stay connected. Doctors could follow along. It gave people in an isolating situation a voice.

I led the UX for that product — and the work was noticed. It directly led to Iternal securing NHS contracts and Innovate UK funding. It also led to a job offer from the NHS itself.

AR storytelling app for children

Secured grant funding to develop an augmented reality storytelling experience, with parallel VR research focused on dementia care.

Designing for children in hospital taught me that good UX isn't just about usability. It's about dignity. The people using your product are often scared, tired, and not there by choice. That changes every design decision.

2 The challenge

Joining an organisation that wasn't ready for change

When I joined NHS Education for Scotland as UX Designer and Researcher, it was 2023 — the year ChatGPT took off. I was already using AI tools daily for content, admin, and ideation. The team I joined was not.

The NHS is a complex, risk-averse institution. Prototyping was done in Axure — slow, siloed, and inaccessible to most stakeholders. AI tools were either unknown or actively distrusted. The culture was one of "we've always done it this way," and that wasn't anyone's fault. It was the environment.

Before

Axure for everything

Prototyping lived in a specialist tool. Slow iteration cycles, high technical barrier, limited stakeholder involvement.

Before

No AI in the workflow

Teams doing manually what AI could assist with: presentations, content, research synthesis, documentation.

3 The approach

Meet people where they are. Show, don't tell.

Forcing adoption doesn't work in the NHS. And lecturing people about AI when they're already stretched doesn't either. I took a different approach: I started with the people who were curious, and I showed them one win at a time.

Rather than rolling out a big AI training programme upfront, I introduced tools at the point of need. Someone building a presentation? I'd show them Gamma — hours of work down to minutes. Someone struggling with content? ChatGPT for a first draft. The ask was never "learn this tool." It was always "try this for this specific thing."

4 The project work

Designing services people actually needed

Alongside the cultural change work, I was leading substantive UX projects. These weren't internal tools for show — they were services that affected how NHS Scotland operated.

Real-time staffing app

Five systems into one

Unified five separate systems for staff tracking, bed management, and incident reporting into a single platform. Reduced operational friction for clinical teams under pressure.

Digital Front Door

Identity management UX research

Conducted research that directly influenced Scottish Government decisions on digital access and identity management across NHS Scotland services.

I also contributed to the design system — building components that would be reused across NHS Scotland's digital estate — and developed template boards to standardise design practice across the UX team.

5 The ripple effect

Internal training that became an external movement

The AI training I started internally at the NHS didn't stay internal. I was speaking at Leeds Digital, running Figma training, delivering UX Scotland recaps to the team. As I learned externally, I brought it back. As I taught internally, I got better at explaining it externally.

That flywheel eventually took me to the main stage at UX Scotland — presenting to over 100 UX professionals on practical AI for user-centred design. From there, the invitations kept coming.

What this taught me about change in large organisations

You can't mandate adoption. Especially not in the NHS, and especially not for technology that people have been told to be cautious about. What you can do is find the path of least resistance — the person who's curious, the task that takes too long, the win that's impossible to argue with.

Showing someone that Gamma can build a presentation in minutes rather than hours isn't a training session. It's a demonstration that their time has value. That's the real argument for AI in any organisation — not capability, but respect for the people doing the work.

Everything that followed — the speaking invitations, the award, the charity work, the 1,000 professionals trained — started with showing one person one tool at the right moment. That's still how I think about change.
