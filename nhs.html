1 The foundation

Designing for children in hospital

My first UX role was at Iternal — building digital patient diaries for children and families during hospital stays.

The idea was simple and human: children could tell their stories, share them with loved ones, and stay connected, while clinicians followed along. It gave people in an isolating situation a voice. I led the UX, and the work was noticed — it helped Iternal secure NHS contracts and Innovate UK funding, and led to a job offer from the NHS itself.

Bristol Children's Hospital

A storytelling platform for children in hospital to share experiences with family, friends, and clinical teams.

Innovate UK funded

AR storytelling app for children

Grant-funded augmented-reality storytelling, with parallel VR research focused on dementia care.

Designing for children in hospital taught me that good UX isn't just usability — it's dignity. When people are scared, tired, and not there by choice, that changes every design decision.

2 The challenge

Joining an organisation that wasn't ready for change

I joined NHS Education for Scotland in 2023 — the year ChatGPT took off. I was already using AI daily; the team wasn't.

The NHS is complex and risk-averse. Prototyping lived in Axure — slow, siloed, inaccessible to most stakeholders — and AI tools were either unknown or actively distrusted. The culture was "we've always done it this way." Not anyone's fault; it was the environment.

Before · Tooling

Axure for everything

Prototyping in a specialist tool meant slow iteration, a high technical barrier, and limited stakeholder involvement.

Before · Workflow

No AI in the workflow

Teams did manually what AI could assist with — presentations, content, research synthesis, documentation.

3 The approach

Meet people where they are. Show, don't tell.

You can't force adoption in the NHS — so I started with the curious, and showed them one win at a time.

Rather than a big AI programme upfront, I introduced tools at the point of need. Someone building a presentation got shown Gamma — hours into minutes. Someone stuck on content got ChatGPT for a first draft. The ask was never "learn this tool," always "try this, for this one thing."

5 The ripple effect

Internal training that became an external movement

The AI training I started inside the NHS didn't stay inside it.

As I learned externally I brought it back; as I taught internally I got better at explaining it externally. That flywheel took me to the main stage at UX Scotland — 100+ UX professionals — and the invitations kept coming.

What this taught me about change in large organisations

You can't mandate adoption — not in the NHS, and not for technology people have been told to be cautious about. What you can do is find the path of least resistance: the person who's curious, the task that takes too long, the win that's impossible to argue with.

Showing someone that Gamma builds a presentation in minutes, not hours, isn't a training session — it's proof that their time has value. That's the real argument for AI in any organisation: not capability, but respect for the people doing the work. The talks, the award, the charity work, the 1,000 professionals trained — all of it started with showing one person one tool at the right moment.
