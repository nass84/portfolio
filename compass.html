My process

I design from real behaviour. On Compass I watched how people actually used the product through Pendo and session recordings, and I started with the power users to learn what "good" looked like before working back to low-use and first-time users. The friction I designed against, people getting lost trying to return to a project, jargon that lost non-researchers, clarifying questions nobody read, all came from watching real sessions rather than guessing.

1 The product

One designer, a new category of tool

Compass is Voxpopme's agentic AI research assistant. It takes a question, searches a customer's research projects, analyses video responses and returns a recommendation.

There was no established design pattern for a tool like this, and as the only designer at Voxpopme I contributed to every layer of the experience: the conversational interface, the onboarding journey, the microcopy, the loading and error states, and the demo content that took it to market.

2 The problem

Customers were drowning in their own data

A beta call with an enterprise customer captured the problem in a single phrase.

They described being faced with a "cloud of selfie videos", hundreds of hours of customer footage with no fast way to find the insight inside it. Compass existed to turn that cloud into an answer, so the design job was to make that feel effortless and trustworthy from the very first session.

A cloud of selfie videos, hundreds of hours of customer footage with no fast way to find the insight inside it.

3 The challenge

Better answers, more friction

Pendo behaviour analysis showed a pattern in first sessions: when Compass opened with a clarifying question before answering, users backed out and returned to plain AI Chat.

Even when the clarification improved answer quality, the perception was that the tool wasn't ready, and first impressions decide whether a habit forms. This surfaced a core tension in agentic AI design, since better answers often require more input, but more input creates friction. I worked with the product team to test whether delaying or removing the clarifying question changed retention, and the recommendation was to default to a best-effort answer first with an option to refine, rather than a mandatory gate.

Loading states and async UX

Every one of those steps is time the user is waiting, so loading states are a UX layer most products ignore, and we didn't. I wrote copy for every state in the processing flow, from progress indicators to waiting messages to "come back later" states for longer tasks. The goal was to reduce anxiety, not just fill silence.

5 Onboarding

Teaching a new behaviour

The Pendo data showed users getting a strong first answer and then immediately creating a project, which meant they missed what actually made Compass valuable.

I rewrote the onboarding so the product taught its own value: an introduction conversation and a product tour that replaced the earlier tutorial, framed around what a user could get out of Compass rather than a feature walk-through.

6 Outcomes

A first month that made the case

Compass's first full month of enterprise usage showed the design was landing.

The majority of users produced a tangible deliverable, most came back without being prompted, and returning users came back repeatedly. Adoption settled into two healthy patterns, wide and shallow across large accounts and narrow and deep with individual power users, which gave the business a clearer picture of who Compass was for.

What I learned designing for agentic AI

Agentic AI tools create new design problems that don't have established patterns. The tension between answer quality and interaction friction is real, since users will choose the tool that responds fastest even when the slower tool gives better answers. Trust is built through transparency, so show your reasoning, through reliability, so don't fail on first use, and through respect for attention, so don't make people wait without a way out.

Designing Compass also reinforced that UX writing is product design. The words in a loading state, an error message or an onboarding tooltip carry the same weight as the layout decisions, and on a solo design team you can't afford to treat them as separate disciplines.
