1 The constraint

One designer. A product org that kept growing.

At Voxpopme I'm the only designer. That means I'm responsible for the full stack: product UX, design systems, brand outputs, presentations, demo content, research analysis, and writing. As the product grew — and as Compass, Voxpopme's agentic AI product, moved from concept to live — the scope grew faster than any one person could keep up with using traditional tools.

The conventional response is to hire. I took a different approach: I changed how I design.

2 The shift

From designing components to designing instructions

The core insight was this: if Claude can generate production-quality code and design outputs, the bottleneck isn't execution — it's the quality of the instructions. A designer who writes excellent prompts and structured skills can operate at a fundamentally different scale than one who doesn't.

I started treating skills the way I'd previously treated design systems: as reusable, high-quality references that encode decisions once and apply them everywhere.

Before
  • Build each component manually in Figma or code
  • Write copy separately, then paste into designs
  • Build presentations slide by slide
  • Re-explain brand rules each time
  • Context-switch constantly between tools
  • Output limited by hours in the day
After
  • Write a skill once; generate components on demand
  • UX copy and design produced in the same session
  • Branded presentations generated from a prompt
  • Brand rules encoded in the skill — applied automatically
  • Claude handles execution; I handle decisions
  • Output limited by clarity of thinking, not time
3 The system

The skills I built and why

A skill is a structured markdown file that encodes design decisions, brand rules, accessibility requirements, and component patterns. Claude reads it before generating any output. Writing a good skill is a design act — it requires the same decisions you'd make building a design system, but expressed in language rather than pixels.

I built and maintain a library of skills covering the full range of Voxpopme outputs:

4 The design decisions

What makes a skill actually work

Writing a skill that consistently produces good output requires the same discipline as writing a design system that developers actually follow. Most fail for the same reason: they're too vague to be actionable, or too prescriptive to handle variation. Here's what I learned.

Encode the reasoning, not just the rule

A rule like "use Dark Teal (#2E7D60) for teal text on white backgrounds" is more durable than "use teal for text" because it explains why — the brand teal (#58B999) fails WCAG contrast at body size. Claude can apply the reasoning to edge cases the rule doesn't anticipate.

Separate brand from behaviour

Brand skills (colours, typography, voice) are stable and change rarely. Behaviour skills (component patterns, output rules) evolve with the product. Keeping them separate means I can update one without breaking the other.

Anti-patterns are as important as patterns

The PPTX skill includes explicit anti-patterns — things that have been misused or look wrong even when technically correct. "Never put white text inside sentiment-coloured chart bars" is a rule that only exists because I'd seen it go wrong. Negative examples are the most reliable guard rails.

Skills are iterable, like any design artefact

The first version of the platform agent skill had no accessibility constraints. After an audit revealed WCAG contrast failures in the brand palette, I rebuilt it with accessible text variants for every background. The skill now encodes that the brand teal fails contrast at small sizes, and maps to a dark variant automatically.

What this says about how design is changing

The question for hiring managers isn't whether a designer uses AI — everyone will. It's whether they understand it well enough to build reliable systems with it, rather than just using it as a faster Figma.

Writing a skill requires you to articulate every design decision you'd normally make implicitly. It forces clarity. A designer who can do that is also a designer who understands their own work at a deeper level — and can communicate it to engineers, product managers, and stakeholders far more effectively than someone who keeps the knowledge in their head.

The output of my workflow isn't just faster design. It's design that's documented, consistent, accessible, and transferable. That's the real shift.
