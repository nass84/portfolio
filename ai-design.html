My process

Writing a skill forces me to make every implicit design decision explicit. My approach is to encode the reasoning rather than just the rule, keep brand separate from behaviour so each can change on its own, and treat every skill as an iterable design artefact that I audit and improve, the same way I would a component library.

1 The constraint

One designer. A product org that kept growing.

I'm the only designer at Voxpopme, responsible for the full stack, for a product that kept outgrowing what one person could ship.

Product UX, design systems, brand, presentations, demo content, research analysis, writing. As Compass, Voxpopme's agentic AI product, moved from concept to live, the scope grew faster than traditional tools could keep up with.

The conventional response is to hire. I changed how I design instead.

2 The shift

From designing components to designing instructions

If Claude can generate production-quality output, the bottleneck isn't execution, it's the quality of the instructions.

So I started treating skills the way I'd treated design systems, as reusable references that encode a decision once and apply it everywhere. I write a skill once, Claude generates from it, and the output covers chat components, branded decks, UX copy, and charts and diagrams.

3 The system

The skills I built and why

A skill is a markdown file that encodes design decisions, brand rules and accessibility requirements. Writing one is a design act, since the decisions are expressed in language rather than pixels.

Claude reads it before generating anything, and I maintain a library covering the full range of Voxpopme outputs.

voxpopme-platform-agent / SKILL.md
## Colour, teal text on light backgrounds
Brand teal #58B999 fails WCAG AA at body size.   ← the reasoning, not just the rule
Map teal text on white to Dark Teal #2E7D60 instead.

## Anti-patterns   ← negative examples are the best guard rails
Never put white text inside sentiment-coloured chart bars.

## Brand tokens   ← one source of truth, reused everywhere
--purple: #341C4C   --teal: #58B999   --teal-text: #2E7D60

A fragment of one skill. The comments mark what makes it reliable: it encodes why, names the anti-patterns, and points at a single set of brand tokens.

voxpopme-copywriting
Brand voice, writing principles, preferred vocabulary, microcopy rules, tone modulation by context. Applied to all written outputs.

Customer archetype system (Truth Teller, Shape Shifter, Future Finder). Identifies archetype from user language and maps to the right product positioning and front door.
4 The design decisions

What makes a skill actually work

Skills fail for the same reason design systems do, so either too vague to act on, or too rigid to handle variation. Here's what makes one reliable.

Encode the reasoning, not just the rule

A rule like "use Dark Teal (#2E7D60) for teal text on white backgrounds" is more durable than "use teal for text" because it explains why, since the brand teal (#58B999) fails WCAG contrast at body size. Claude can then apply the reasoning to edge cases the rule doesn't anticipate.

Aa Sample text
Brand teal #58B9992.4:1 · Fails AA
Aa Sample text
Dark teal #2E7D605.0:1 · Passes AA

Separate brand from behaviour

Brand skills (colours, typography, voice) are stable and change rarely, while behaviour skills (component patterns, output rules) evolve with the product. Keeping them separate means I can update one without breaking the other.

Anti-patterns matter as much as patterns

The PPTX skill includes explicit anti-patterns, things that have been misused or look wrong even when technically correct. "Never put white text inside sentiment-coloured chart bars" only exists because I'd seen it go wrong, and negative examples are the most reliable guard rails.

Skills are iterable, like any design artefact

The first version of the platform agent skill had no accessibility constraints. After an audit revealed WCAG contrast failures in the brand palette, I rebuilt it with accessible text variants for every background, so it now encodes that the brand teal fails contrast at small sizes and maps to a dark variant automatically.

5 In practice

What this enabled

Skill-driven generation changed what fits in a working week.

Decks

A branded deck from a prompt

A fully branded Voxpopme presentation, correct fonts, colours, sentiment chart system and layouts, generated from a brief in a single session. Previously half a day's work, now 20 minutes of review.

Systems

A design system at speed

The platform agent design system covers 53 chat components. Building those manually would have taken weeks, writing the skill took two days, and every component since has stayed on brand without me touching it.

Writing

Copy and design together

UX copy for Compass, loading states, error messages, onboarding and microcopy, produced in the same session as layout. The copywriting skill applies the voice automatically, so I review rather than write from scratch.

What this says about how design is changing

The question for hiring managers isn't whether a designer uses AI, everyone will. It's whether they understand it well enough to build reliable systems with it, rather than just using it as a faster Figma.

Writing a skill requires you to articulate every design decision you'd normally make implicitly, which forces clarity. A designer who can do that understands their own work at a deeper level, and can communicate it to engineers, product managers and stakeholders far more effectively than someone who keeps the knowledge in their head. The output of my workflow isn't just faster design, it's design that's documented, consistent, accessible and transferable, and that's the real shift.
