1 Why I joined

This one was personal before it was professional

Before moving into tech, I spent years at Citizens Advice working directly with people experiencing homelessness. I understood the system's failures from the inside: the endless form-filling, the mis-entries, the placements that made a crisis worse. When I heard about a government hackathon to tackle homelessness through AI, joining wasn't a career decision. It was an obvious one.

The brief was open: use AI to make a meaningful difference in housing. We chose to focus on temporary accommodation matching — a critical, high-stakes process that was still largely manual, and failing people because of it.

2 The problem

12,000 officer hours. Still producing bad matches.

Based on Sheffield City Council's data, councils handle around 6,000 accommodation matches per year. Each manual match takes roughly two hours: pulling together needs assessments, checking property records, cross-referencing risk information. That's 12,000 officer hours annually — the equivalent of 7.3 full-time staff — just on matching.

And it still goes wrong. Risk data gets mis-entered. Properties get assigned that are unsuitable or unsafe. Officers start again. Every failure costs time the system doesn't have, and puts a vulnerable person — often a family in crisis — in the wrong place.

3 The human cost

What a bad match looks like in practice

Composite case — based on real experiences

Shaheen arrived at the council at 9am with her three children, fleeing domestic violence. By 1:30pm she'd been offered a property 3 miles from her danger zone — because risk data had been mis-entered. The match was unsafe.

Back to searching. By 3pm a second option: 45 minutes from her support network, 90 minutes from her children's schools. When she arrived, it was a B&B housing adult men.

She called the out-of-hours line. No alternatives. She paid for a hotel that night herself.

This isn't an edge case. It's what the system produces when it's under pressure and running on manual processes. The cost isn't just financial — every bad match is a family in the wrong place at the worst possible moment.

4 The solution

Four steps to replace a two-hour manual process

MATCH replaces the manual workflow with a four-stage AI-assisted process. Crucially, it keeps the officer in control at every step — AI handles the information processing, humans make the placement decision.

01

Voice recording

Officer records the needs assessment verbally. No forms. Natural conversation.

02

Auto-transcription

Audio transcribed and key information extracted: needs, risks, exclusion zones.

03

Weighted matching

Algorithm scores available properties against the household profile. Transparent, not black-box.

04

Officer review

Ranked results with reasoning. Officer makes the final call with full context.

The matching uses a weighted scoring algorithm — deliberately interpretable and transparent rather than a machine learning model. That matters for trust, for auditability, and for explaining decisions to households and officers.

5 My contribution

Design decisions I owned

As the UX designer on the team, my role was to make sure the prototype was built around the people who'd actually use it — not just technically functional. That meant driving the design decisions and leading the user research.

Decision
Reasoning
Voice recording instead of forms
Officers already do verbal assessments. Recording captures the natural conversation rather than forcing a new behaviour.
Weighted algorithm, not ML model
Interpretable decisions build trust. Officers need to explain placements to households — a black box makes that impossible.
Officer makes the final placement
The system removes the processing work, not the human judgement. Critical for safeguarding and accountability.
Ranked results with reasoning shown
Officers need to understand why a property was ranked highly, not just that it was. Transparency reduces errors and builds confidence.
6 User research

We won the hackathon — then started the real work

Winning was the beginning, not the end. Before making any further improvements to the prototype, I insisted on proper user research. Voxpopme gave us access to their platform for free, which let us speak to three distinct groups — something most hackathon projects never do.

Group 1

People with lived experience of homelessness

Understanding the emotional reality of the matching process from the household's perspective. What felt dehumanising. What built trust. What they needed officers to know.

Group 2

Local authority service users

People who had navigated the council housing system. Their experiences shaped our understanding of where the process breaks down in practice.

Group 3

Frontline caseworkers

The primary users of the tool. Their feedback on the prototype was direct and practical — and shaped improvements in the next iteration.

Caseworkers told us the prototype could remove the parts of the job that ate their time without adding value — the cross-referencing, the data entry, the re-searching after a failed match. They wanted to spend that time with the people in front of them, not in spreadsheets.

7 Outcome

From hackathon to Parliament

The prototype won the government hackathon outright. Three months later I presented it to MPs in Parliament — standing up to explain both the problem and what we'd built, to an audience that could actually act on it.

That required a different kind of design work: translating a technical prototype into a narrative that landed for a policy audience. I wrote the full presentation structure, speaker notes for a 13-slide deck, and the video research intro script.

Gov.uk Hackathon — First Place

Competing against multidisciplinary teams from across government and the third sector. MATCH was selected as the standout prototype for its combination of technical rigour, genuine user insight, and real-world applicability.

Presented to MPs, Parliament — March 2026

Invited to present the prototype to Members of Parliament as part of the government's commitment to reviewing solutions from the hackathon cohort. The first time the MATCH prototype had a direct line to policy decision-makers.

What I took from this

The projects that matter most are rarely the ones that start with a clean brief and a full team. MATCH started in 48 hours with people I'd never met, solving a problem I cared about long before I worked in tech.

The design decisions that held up under scrutiny — voice recording, interpretable algorithms, officer control — weren't clever ideas. They came directly from understanding the people using the system. The research we did after winning mattered as much as anything built during the hackathon itself.

Presenting to MPs was a reminder that design work has a life beyond the screen. The prototype earned that room. The research made the argument credible. Getting both right is the job.
